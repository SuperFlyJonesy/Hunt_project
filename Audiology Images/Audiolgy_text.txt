Types of Hearing Loss
Sensorineural hearing loss
- Caused by damage to the sensitive hair
cells inside the inner ear, or damage to
the auditory nerve
- Commonly seen as ‘age-related’ hearing
loss

Conductive hearing loss
- Sounds are unable to pass from the outer
ear to the inner ear
- Can be caused by a blockage inside the
middle ear

Types of Hearing Loss
Mild
- Difficulty hearing speech in more difficult hearing environments

Moderate
- Difficulty hearing speech when not facing, or speaking loudly and clearly

Severe
- Difficulty hearing a lot unless lip-reading, or focusing hard

Profound
- Most sounds unable to be heard, speech unintelligible, aid required

Hearing Aids
BTE - ‘behind the ear’ hearing
aids which are the most
common type of hearing aid

ITE - ‘in the ear’ hearing aids
that are small enough to sit in the
ear

They are normally held in the
ear by an earmould or thintube
and dome

ITC – ‘in the canal’ hearing aids
which sit farther into the ear

Body worn hearing aids - not as common
as other hearing aids, and are more often
worn by very elderly or dementia patients
with a profound hearing loss
They have a microphone and ear receivers
that are quite far apart, so there is less
chance of feedback

Hearing Aids
Bone Conducting Aid
This may be given to patients who have a conductive
deafness

They do not sit on the ear but behind on the bone

BAHA (Bone
Anchored
Hearing Aid)
A metal stud is
surgically
implanted into
the mastoid
bone to which a
bone-conductor
is attached to
convert sound
into vibration

Cochlear Implant
A device which is surgically implanted
into the cochlea that destroys the ear but
supplants the sounds normally heard
with electrical stimulation of the cochlea
An external processor also needs to be
worn to function
Implanted from 5-6 months old up to 90+
years old

Referrals to NHS Audiology
GP
- Will look in your ears, ask about hearing + other
symptoms
- Tuning fork test, or whisper test
- May not ask where you’d like to go for a hearing test
ENT
- NHS and private ENT can refer to audiology
- Tinnitus, balance, hearing aids

Hearing Aid Maintenance
Knowing how to keep a hearing aid working could be the difference between comfortable
hearing and social isolation
Maintenance includes:
- Changing the batteries
- Keeping the earmoulds clear
- Retubing earmoulds
- Replacing thintubes
