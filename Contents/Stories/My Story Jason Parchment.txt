## My Story: Jason Parchment

### The Early Years and First Operations

I lost my hearing around the age of 30. Looking back, I think it came down to a combination of things: spending too much time in nightclubs and bars listening to heavy music right up close to the speakers, alongside a slight hearing issue I had when I was young that always left me prone to infections.

In my late 20s and early 30s, I found out I had a cholesteatoma—a benign tumor that sits behind the eardrum. If it's not removed, it can cause irreparable damage to the inner ear, so I had to undergo operations on both ears.

My right ear surgery was done first, right here in Bristol at the Bristol Royal Infirmary (BRI). It was an older type of operation and quite invasive. They performed a mastoidectomy, removing the mastoid, and I lost about 50% of my hearing on that side. They took out my entire eardrum and performed a skin graft, taking a piece of skin from underneath my scalp near my temple to create a new eardrum. It left a large cavity behind, meaning it doesn't really fit the normal-sized domes you get on standard hearing aids. Over the years, that side has gotten worse; it's constantly prone to "gunking" up, which eventually led to another operation because of an infection.

My left side involved a different type of procedure. By then, medical techniques had evolved into micro-operations, allowing them to approach it differently so that my mastoid bones behind the ear could remain somewhat intact. I navigated operations on that side through my 30s and into my late 30s, and later had another operation in London at the Royal National Throat, Nose and Ear Hospital.

Later on, my left ear required another operation due to an infection, which meant turning that side into a mastoid cavity as well to clear it out. Going through these operations meant facing some heavy risks. I remember having to sign waivers acknowledging that if they accidentally cut my facial nerve, I could lose movement on both sides of my face and suffer facial drop. Thankfully the operations were successful, but my face didn't look the same afterward. My right ear protrudes much more, and my left ear is slightly sunken into my face. It took me a few years to truly come to terms with it.

### Overcoming the Stigma and Finding the First Aid

Because of how everything looked and felt, I flat-out refused to have any kind of hearing aid right up until I hit 45. When I moved to Derbyshire, a hospital there finally convinced me to try a hearing aid on my right side—just a big, normal one from the NHS.

It completely changed my life. For nearly 15 years, I had been struggling to understand people behind me, constantly guessing, making little noises, and developing physical ticks just to mask the difficulty and avoid embarrassment. Overcoming the stigma attached to hearing aids took a lot of time. At first, I mostly wore it at home, where my partner made a world of difference in helping me adjust. Living with this means that when I sleep, or when I take my hearing aids out and go horizontal, my hearing drops off completely—I'm totally deaf.

### Modern Care and the Tech Revolution

When I moved back to Bristol during COVID, I needed to get a fresh assessment. After going to Specsavers, I was told I should have aids on both sides. After almost a year of suffering from clogged ears and infections, I managed to get a referral to St. Michael's Hospital Audiology department, and during my fitting, the lady managed to speak to someone in the ENT department for them to take a look at my ears. Since then, I’ve had a few CAT scans and MRIs to check if the bone has grown over or if any of the small bones aren't working as they should.

Despite the ongoing medical hurdles, I fully embraced my hearing journey in early 2024. Today, I have two rechargeable Phonak Bolero MPR model hearing aids, and they have genuinely changed my life. I can hear properly on both sides, stream music, and answer telephone calls seamlessly. It’s always a funny experience sitting in a social gathering—like a pub or restaurant—getting a phone call, and just answering it directly through my hearing aids while people around me wonder who on earth I'm talking to until I explain!

Music is a massive part of my life now, and having it 24/7 helps me manage my chronic tinnitus. I can have ambient music playing in the background, listen to an audiobook while sitting in a waiting room without needing extra earphones, or go shopping and stream music straight from my smartwatch. I've got about three different pairs of hearing aids now, and I've just gotten a new mold on my right side to see how it feels.

A major highlight was getting involved with Auracast technology. While working for the Centre for Deaf and Hard of Hearing People, I was lucky enough to take part in a public trial at Bristol Temple Meads station. Getting down there to see the system in action and experiencing crisp audio streamed directly was incredible—it was a true "my God, it actually works" moment.

### The Hidden Realities of Daily Life

Living with this type of hearing loss comes with daily practical challenges that most fully hearing people never think about. Restaurants, echoey public places, and meetings can be exhausting. If someone speaks quietly, has a low pitch, covers their mouth, or if there's chatter coming from the next room, it becomes an uphill battle to catch the words.

There's a delicate balance to the technology, too. If I turn my hearing aids up to full, I feel like Superman—I can hear sounds from across the world—but it hurts, causes headaches, and quickly becomes uncomfortable. Furthermore, my ears need to breathe to prevent infections, which flare up much worse during the summer months or when the seasons change. Because of my surgical cavities, managing everyday life means taking natural precautions: wearing earplugs when swimming and being careful even when taking a morning shower.

Ironically, most people don't even realize I wear hearing aids. I’ve got small, discreet black ones that match my hair, and even when I worked alongside deaf colleagues, they would occasionally forget I have a hearing loss. Sometimes taking the aids out for a bit of quiet seems tempting, but the trade-off is brutal: the chronic tinnitus comes rushing back louder than ever, which is never a pleasant experience.

### Navigating Identity, Grief, and the Future

For those of us who lose our hearing later in life, the journey feels much more like a grieving process than stepping into a shared cultural identity like it is for the profoundly deaf. I didn’t want to lose my hearing, and I certainly didn’t want my hearing loss to define me. For a long time, I found it very difficult to classify myself as disabled, even though the government and council recognize me as such—qualifying me for support, PIP, and Access to Work.

For 20 to 25 years, I didn’t use any of those resources because I simply couldn't acknowledge that I had a hearing loss. I kept my head in the sand and lived in denial. But coming to terms with it over the past couple of years changed everything. My hearing aids don't give me back my original hearing, but they give me a dramatically better life experience. I’ve even started looking at myself as augmented—kind of like a cyborg or a robot!

Everyone's medical landscape is different, too. My high frequencies are gone on my left side, while my low frequencies are gone on my right, accompanied by the medical baggage of dizziness and balance issues stemming from my surgeries.

There is still so much baggage attached to hearing aids. It reminds me of the old schoolyard days when kids used to get teased as "four eyes" for wearing glasses—traumas that stay with us right into adulthood. People still carry this archaic stigma that hearing aids make you look slow, dumb, or "deaf and dumb." It's crazy. But just like glasses became completely normal and stylish, we are reaching a turning point where hearing aids can be the same. This is our chance.

When it comes to community, hard-of-hearing people don't have the same shared, foundational culture or native language that the profoundly deaf community shares. British Sign Language (BSL) is fantastic and trendy—I've completed Level 1 and know a good amount of it, which is genuinely useful—but I will never be profoundly deaf. My path is about losing a sense I once had. It's a different journey, and we all deal with varying levels of loss.

The future of hearing technology is genuinely thrilling. I am deeply interested in how innovations like Auracast are going to fundamentally change public spaces, theaters, and auditoriums, making them accessible to everyone. We're also seeing artificial intelligence evolve inside hearing aids, holding massive promise for helping us better understand and manage conditions like chronic tinnitus.

Now, my goal is to use what I've been through to help others. I'm starting right here in Bristol with a hearing loss initiative to support people locally, with hopes of eventually expanding to other cities and maybe even going global. I want to guide people through the process, help them avoid the years of denial I went through, and show them that while losing your hearing is a heavy thing to process, you don't have to face it alone.

### A Unique Journey for Everyone

Perhaps you have a similar story. As I always say, everybody's loss is different. If you want to share your stories with us, I think it's good for us to understand that it's not the same for everybody. Our hearing is unique, and the devices and help that work for us are different, too. There is no one-size-fits-all fix; it is a completely individual experience.

In fact, your hearing profile can vary just as widely as any fully hearing person's. Some people I know can hear a pin drop on the other side of the room, while others struggle to hear themselves breathing heavily.

We are here to support and help. I hope you find my journey helpful in some ways, and I look forward to sharing and connecting with yours.
